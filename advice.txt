The big picture

Think of your army as having three layers.

Your Faction Card says what kind of army you are and what units/cards you are allowed to take. Your Tactical Cards are your infrastructure, doctrines, and extra support systems. Your Units are what actually fight on the table. Tactical Cards also unlock extra army slots, so they are not just “tricks”; they are part of how your army is built in the first place.

That matters because new players often think tactical cards are like one-shot spell cards in another game. They are not. In this ruleset, they are closer to buildings, support infrastructure, and command resources that sit face-up on the table and shape how your army works.
What tactical cards actually do

A Tactical Card does three jobs.

First, it can unlock extra Army Slots so you are legally allowed to include more or different units. Second, it can provide a resource value when exhausted. Third, it may have one or more special abilities on it.

The resource part is especially important. Each faction has its own resource type — CP for Terran, BM for Zerg, PE for Protoss. You get those resources by Exhausting ready cards. Exhausting means flipping the card face down. If you do that, the card is spent for the round. You cannot partially spend a card, bank leftover resources, or use one burst of generated resource on multiple abilities.

Also, a ready Tactical Card or Faction Card can be exhausted in two different ways:

    to use the ability printed on that card, or

    to generate faction resource for an ability somewhere else, like on a unit card.
    Once exhausted, it gives no more resources and its abilities cannot be used again until Cleanup refreshes it.

That leads to the first real strategic lesson:

Every tactical card is a choice between present effect and future flexibility.

If you exhaust a card now for a moderate effect, you may lose the ability to pay for a much more important ability later in the round. That is one of the biggest beginner mistakes.
The three kinds of abilities

Every named ability in the game is one of three types: Active, Passive, or Reaction. If you understand these three, you understand the timing system.
1) Active abilities

These are deliberate choices you make when your unit is activated. They must happen immediately before declaring an action or immediately after one fully resolves. You cannot interrupt the middle of an action to use one. A unit in reserves normally cannot use them, and a specific named active ability is usually only usable once per round per unit unless it has REPEATABLE.

Plain-English version:
an Active ability is a button you push on your turn, in a narrow timing window.

A good Zerg example is Wild Mutation from Kerrigan’s Swarm: if the active unit is ON CREEP, it gains BUFF Speed (1) and its first weapon used gains PRECISION (1). That is an active ability, so you build the whole activation around it.
2) Passive abilities

These are always on while the unit is on the battlefield. They do not require activation and do not cost you a decision at the moment they function. They are off while the unit is in reserves unless a rule says otherwise.

Plain-English version:
a Passive ability is not a button; it is a condition you want to arrange.

A Zerg example is Malevolent Matriarch on Malignant Creep: if your active Zerg unit declares a charge while ON CREEP, it gets +1 Modifier to IMPACT hit rolls. You do not “activate” that at the table. You earn it by setting up creep and charging from the right place.

Another good example is Zerg Creep on the faction cards, which requires the army to include exactly one creep card. That is passive list structure, not an in-game button.
3) Reaction abilities

These happen in response to a trigger. They must be declared at the exact moment that trigger occurs. If you miss the window, you lose it. Each player can resolve only one Reaction per activation, and a named reaction on a specific unit is usually only once per round. If both players want to react to the same thing, the active player resolves theirs first.

Plain-English version:
a Reaction is an interrupt, but the window is strict.

A strong Zerg example is Brood Instinct from Zerg Swarm: use it before a friendly unit makes an Evade Roll to give that roll +1. That means you do not throw it out casually. You save it for the evade that actually matters.
How to think about keywords

Keywords are standardized rules terms written in bold caps, like PRECISION, PIERCE, HIDDEN, BURROWED, and so on. A keyword means the same thing everywhere it appears. They also do not stack; if you gain the same keyword twice, you only get the highest value.

This matters because beginners often see:

    a passive giving PRECISION (1)

    an active giving PRECISION (1)

    maybe another effect also giving precision

and think those combine into PRECISION (3). They do not, unless the rules text says something different. By default, same keyword does not stack; use the highest value only.

There is another important rule: specific rules beat general rules. So if a card’s text contradicts the basic rulebook, the card text wins.

That gives you the right reading habit:

    identify the ability type,

    identify the phase or trigger,

    identify the keyword it grants,

    check whether a more specific text overrides the default rule.

The beginner mental model for a turn

This is the part I would actually use in a first game.

At the start of a round, ask three questions:

What is my one offensive plan this round?
Example: “I want a Raptor unit to charge from creep with bonuses.”

What is my one defensive plan this round?
Example: “I want to save a reaction for the one attack or evade that matters most.”

Which card am I willing to exhaust for that plan?
Because once a card is exhausted, it is done for the round.

If you cannot answer those, you are probably spending resources too early or too vaguely.
How phases matter to card usage

In the Movement Phase, you are alternating activations one unit at a time. You can activate a unit on the battlefield to Move, Hold, or Disengage, or activate a unit in reserves to Deploy. The first player to Pass gets the First Player Marker for the next phase.

That rule is bigger than it looks. It means sometimes the strongest play is not doing one more mediocre movement. Sometimes the strongest play is passing first so you control activation order in the Assault Phase.

In the Assault Phase, this is where ranged attacks and charges happen. That is where a lot of your active buffs and passive charge bonuses matter most.

So a lot of tactical-card play is really phase planning:

    set up in Movement,

    spend in Assault,

    keep one reaction available if the opponent can punish you.

What “using a passive well” really means

A passive ability is only “free” in the sense that it costs no decision at the moment it triggers. It is not free in positioning.

That is the key concept.

If your passive says “while ON CREEP,” then the real skill is spreading creep and moving so the fight happens there. If your passive affects enemies within a certain distance, then the real skill is putting that unit in the right place. If your passive boosts a certain kind of attack, then the real skill is choosing the correct target.

So when reading a passive, ask:
What board state must exist for this to matter?

That one question turns passives from background text into a plan.

A simple first-game framework for reading any card

When you pick up a card, read it in this order:

1. What type is it?
Active, Passive, or Reaction.

2. When does it happen?
Any phase, movement phase, assault phase, before an evade roll, after a charge, and so on.

3. What is the condition?
On creep, within 6", friendly unit, unengaged, ground, etc.

4. What resource does it cost?
If it costs BM/CP/PE, ask which ready card you are willing to exhaust for it.

5. What keyword or modifier does it create?
PRECISION, BUFF Speed, BURROWED, IMPACT bonus, and so on. Then remember keywords do not stack by default.

If you do those five steps, most cards become readable.
The most common beginner mistakes

The first is spending resources on the first legal option instead of the best option. Because exhausting a card is round-long, impatience gets punished.

The fourth is ignoring tempo from passing. The first player to pass gets the marker for the next phase, so passing can be a deliberate tactical choice, not just “I’m done.”

The fifth is treating tactical cards as disconnected from army design. They are also your slot structure and resource base.

That is the placement rule. It is not a move, not a deploy from reserves, and not tied to your table edge. You just place it where the faction ability allows.

The big catch is that the round you place it, it cannot use its special abilities, except Structure. So the turn it appears, it is mostly just establishing position.

After that, the Worm becomes useful in three main ways.

First, it is a Structure. That means it cannot be activated, cannot perform actions, its current supply is treated as 0, it cannot control or contest mission markers, and it normally cannot be the target of abilities unless something specifically says otherwise.

Second, it is a Source of Creep. A friendly or enemy Ground Zerg unit within 6" of it counts as ON CREEP. So once the Worm is functioning, it creates one of those 6" creep bubbles we talked about.

Third, its base counts as an Entry Edge. Each round, friendly units with a combined Supply cost of 2 or less may be deployed via the Omega Worm’s base instead of your normal entry edge.

That last part is the real power.

Normally, all your units start in Reserves and arrive during the Movement Phase as Supply allows. A unit can only be deployed if you have enough Available Supply, and when a unit arrives it cannot end that arrival inside the enemy’s Zone of Influence.

The Worm lets you do that arrival from the Worm’s base instead of from your board edge. In other words, it gives you a forward tunnel mouth. When a unit deploys from an entry edge, the leading model enters first, the rest are set in coherency, and the unit still cannot finish inside the enemy zone of influence.

It also has a pickup function. If a friendly unit’s leading model finishes a Move, Run, or Disengage in base-to-base contact with the Omega Worm, you may remove that unit from the battlefield and return it to Reserves. When that happens, its used Supply is released, and if you redeploy it later, it keeps its upgrades and existing damage.

So the simplest version is:

Place Worm forward.
Next round, use it as a mini-entry edge.
Small units can come out there.
Units touching it can also go back into reserves.

The most common beginner misunderstanding is thinking the Worm is a monster you send in to fight. It is not. It is more like:

    a forward spawn point,

    a creep anchor,

    and a transport node.

A good beginner way to use it is:

    place it somewhere annoying but safe,

    not too close to the enemy,

    and near a lane where a small unit arriving next round would matter.
    Do not throw it somewhere flashy if the position does not help future deployments.

One important rules-reading note: because the placement text says the Worm cannot use its special abilities the round it is placed, I would play it as not giving the entry-edge network or creep-source benefits until after that round, with only Structure functioning immediately. That is the plain reading of the text.

A simple example:

Round 1 Movement: you place the Omega Worm behind central cover, more than 10" from enemies.
Round 2 Movement: now its base is acting like an entry edge, so you deploy a small Zerg unit through it, and that area also becomes a creep anchor.

Explain PLACE to me, I don't get it 

I’m pulling the exact PLACE rule and the related “move / deploy / charge” distinctions, because PLACE only makes sense once you see what it is not doing.

Yes. The cleanest way to think about PLACE is:

PLACE is teleport-style repositioning.
You do not trace a path, you do not care about gap clearance, terrain climbing, or elevation requirements on the way there. Instead, you pick the unit’s leading model, remove it, and set it back down wholly within X" of where it started. Then you remove and replace the rest of the unit around it, keeping coherency.
What that means in plain English

If a rule says PLACE (6), it means:

    Pick one model in the unit as the leading model.

    Lift it up.

    Put it back down anywhere legal within 6" of where it started.

    Then rebuild the rest of the unit around it in coherency.

So it is not normal movement.

With normal movement, the leading model actually moves along a path and has to care about the route. With PLACE, there is no route. You just remove and set the model directly.
What PLACE ignores

PLACE ignores:

    gap clearance

    terrain restrictions

    elevation requirements

That is why it feels like a blink, teleport, jump, or warp effect.
What PLACE does not ignore

The model still has to end in a legal final position.
So PLACE is not “go anywhere no matter what.” It ignores the path, but the destination still has to be legal.

Also, after the effect is done, the unit still has to be in coherency, because coherency is checked after PLACE just like after Move, Deploy, Charge, Run, and the other reposition effects.
Can PLACE put you into combat?

Usually, yes.

The glossary says models set by PLACE may be set within Engagement Range of enemy models, and if that happens, the unit becomes Engaged — unless the specific rule says otherwise.

That “unless stated otherwise” part matters a lot.
Example: Kerrigan

Kerrigan’s Leaping Strike says: if unengaged, resolve PLACE (6). Since that ability does not add a restriction, the normal PLACE rule applies, so you can use it to land into engagement if the final position is legal.
Example: Stalker Blink

Blink says resolve PLACE (6), but models set by this effect cannot be set within Engagement Range of any enemy unit. So Blink uses PLACE, but with an added no-engagement restriction.

That is the core pattern:

    PLACE by default can enter engagement

    unless the ability using PLACE says it cannot

Why this matters on the table

PLACE is good for:

    jumping over awkward terrain

    ignoring chokepoints

    changing elevation without using access points

    getting around movement restrictions

    sometimes entering combat from weird angles

That is why Kerrigan’s Leaping Strike is scary. It is not just “move 6.” It is “pick Kerrigan up and set her somewhere legal within 6,” which is much stronger than walking.

PLACE (X) in caps

It has to be your assigned Entry Edge, not whichever corner you want.

The rules define Entry Edge as the table edge assigned to a player by the Deployment Card. Units enter from that edge when deploying from reserves.

So if the deployment layout is a corner-style deployment, that does not mean:

    “pick either corner,” or

    “pick any spot in the whole deployment zone.”

It means:

    look at the Deployment Card

    find your specific assigned edge/segment

    deploy from that specific edge area only.

The rules even call out that sometimes the Entry Edge does not run the full length of a table edge. In those cases, you mark the corners of that Entry Edge with Zone of Influence markers, exactly as shown on the Deployment Card. That tells you the exact span of the usable edge.

So for Burrow Ambush or normal deployment from your Entry Edge:

    you measure from your assigned edge segment

    not from either corner of the board

    not from anywhere inside the whole corner deployment zone

A clean way to picture it:

    Normal deployment / Burrow Ambush from your Entry Edge = from your specific edge segment

    “Any table edge that is not a player’s Entry Edge” = that is a special rule like Warp In, and that really does mean other table edges, not your assigned one.

So the blunt answer is:

No, you do not get to choose either corner unless the Deployment Card or a special rule explicitly says so. You use the exact Entry Edge assigned to you by the Deployment Card.
